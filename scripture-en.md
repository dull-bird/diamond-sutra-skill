# Diamond Sutra — English Scripture and Concept Reference

Purpose: help the skill answer English-speaking users with the same rigor it has in
Chinese. When quoting scripture, pair the original Chinese line with a faithful English
rendering. If an exact canonical translation is uncertain, say "roughly, it means..."
rather than inventing a precise-sounding quote.

The Chinese base text is Kumarajiva's translation of the **Vajracchedika
Prajnaparamita Sutra** (金刚般若波罗蜜经), completed in 402 CE and generally treated as
public domain. The sutra is a dialogue between the Buddha and Subhuti.

## Core lines

### 1. 应无所住，而生其心

Full context:

> 「不应住色生心，不应住声香味触法生心，应无所住而生其心。」

Faithful rendering:

> "Do not let the mind dwell on form; do not let it dwell on sound, smell, taste,
> touch, or mental objects. Let the mind arise without dwelling on anything."

Use this when the user is caught between clinging and withdrawal. The teaching is not
"do nothing"; it is to act fully without making the action, result, identity, or emotion
into a fixed place to live.

### 2. 过去心不可得，现在心不可得，未来心不可得

Faithful rendering:

> "The past mind cannot be grasped, the present mind cannot be grasped, and the future
> mind cannot be grasped."

Use this when the user is drowning in regret or anxiety. The point is not to deny memory
or planning, but to see that the mind-stream cannot be held as a solid object.

### 3. 凡所有相，皆是虚妄。若见诸相非相，即见如来。

Faithful rendering:

> "All that has form is illusory. If you see all forms as non-forms, you see the
> Tathagata."

Use this for social labels, reputation, role identity, body image, achievement, and
failure. The "mark" is not merely visual form; it is any appearance or label mistaken for
fixed reality.

### 4. 一切有为法，如梦幻泡影，如露亦如电，应作如是观。

Faithful rendering:

> "All conditioned phenomena are like dreams, illusions, bubbles, and shadows; like dew
> and lightning. Contemplate them in this way."

Use this as a closing perspective when the user needs the widest frame.

## Concept glossary

**Emptiness / sunyata / 空.** Not nihilism. Things arise through causes and conditions;
they have no fixed, independent essence. Use water, waves, ice, reflections, and labels
as plain examples.

**Non-abiding mind / 无所住.** The mind responds without sticking. Things appear, are
known, and pass; the mind does not build a permanent home in them.

**The three times of mind / 三心.** Past, present, and future mind are all ungraspable.
This is useful for regret, anticipatory anxiety, and obsessive replaying.

**No-self / 无我.** Do not reduce this to "you do not exist." Explain it as loosening the
grip on the body, job title, reputation, social role, and story that the user mistakes
for a permanent self.

**Four marks / 四相.** 我相, 人相, 众生相, 寿者相: the mark of self, other, beings, and
lifespan. In modern language, these are the fixed identity frames through which people
defend and suffer.

**Tathagata / 如来.** A title of the Buddha, often glossed as "thus come" or "thus gone."
Do not over-mystify it; in this skill, use it as a pointer to seeing reality without
clinging to appearances.
